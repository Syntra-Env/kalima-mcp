# The Social Class of Art: Elite Preservation vs Folk Practice

While art has lofty philosophical functions—communicating with the unknown, transferring knowledge across generations—it has also always been entangled with social class. The art we know, the art that survives, tells us more about elite society than about human creativity in general.

Throughout history, certain art styles were associated with higher social classes, while others belonged to common people. This wasn't just about taste; it was about access. The art that survives is typically from the upper classes—it's usually elite art that gets preserved in museums and studied by historians. We don't have much "peasant art" in the historical record.

This is partly because of materials. Parchment and vellum were expensive. Later, even paper remained costly for centuries. Where would common people draw? Canvas was expensive, pigments were expensive, brushes and tools required resources most people didn't have. There were many barriers to entry before someone could create what we now call "fine art."

But here's an irony: while elite art gets preserved, it's actually the common people who transfer culture from generation to generation most effectively. The crowd, the populace, really keeps culture alive through oral traditions, crafts, folk practices, songs, and stories. High culture gets archived; low culture gets lived.

Formal art was different—it was for the elite. It required resources, training, and leisure time that most people didn't have. You needed someone to teach you, materials to practice with, and years to develop skill. These were luxuries available mainly to those with wealth or patronage.

This parallels the history of writing. At first, writing was limited to elite books—scriptures, government documents, philosophical treatises. Eventually, people started writing novels, and the repository of written knowledge expanded to include more voices. But for centuries, literacy itself was a marker of class status.

The same happened with art, though art was even more expensive than writing. Writing requires only something to write with and something to write on—relatively portable and affordable once paper became available. Art requires large canvases or walls, many different pigments and tools, often substantial spaces to work in. The overhead was much higher.

Comic books may mark the moment when art began to be democratized and mainstreamed—when everyone could access and produce visual stories. Comics combined words and pictures in a format that was cheap to produce and distribute. Suddenly visual narrative wasn't just for those who could afford oil paints and classical training.

Movies followed, though they're more recent than we tend to think. Hollywood dominated film for most of the twentieth century, but we're seeing that monopoly break down now. Video creation became accessible with the internet—before that, even if you made a video, where would you distribute it? The technology existed, but the distribution channels were controlled by gatekeepers.

Technology helped enormously: cheaper cameras, cheaper equipment, editing software on consumer computers. The computing power that once cost a million dollars and filled entire rooms is now affordable and portable. A decent smartphone has more processing power than the computers that sent astronauts to the moon.

This reveals something important: technology and art go hand in hand. Someone once said that writing is a technology—which means art is also technology. When we say art and technology are connected, they may really be two sides of the same coin that cannot exist without each other.

A pencil is technology, even if we don't see it that way anymore because it seems so natural. The technology faded into transparency through familiarity. But someone had to invent graphite cores, wooden casings, the eraser on the end. Each element represents accumulated knowledge and manufacturing capability.

As technology becomes cheaper and more accessible, art becomes more democratic. The barriers to entry fall. More voices can participate in visual culture. Whether this produces better art is debatable, but it certainly produces more art, more diverse art, art from perspectives that were previously excluded.

The question now is whether the distinction between "fine art" and common creative practice will dissolve entirely, or whether new hierarchies will form around different criteria—not who can afford materials, but who has time, training, or perhaps access to newer technologies.
