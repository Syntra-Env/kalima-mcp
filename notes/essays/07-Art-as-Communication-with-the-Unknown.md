# Art as Communication with the Unknown

Beyond teaching what's already known, fine art serves another function that's harder to define but equally important: it's a way of communicating with the unknown. Artists don't just record reality; they reach toward something that doesn't yet have form, trying to catch it and bring it here.

This requires vision in the deepest sense. Artists are visionaries—they have to be if they want to succeed at capturing something that doesn't yet exist in the visible world. They're not just copying what they see; they're manifesting what they somehow sense or intuit before anyone else can perceive it.

This process is like being in a dream, trying to grasp something hovering at the edge of consciousness. The artist feels something there—an idea, an image, a mood, a truth—and works to make it concrete. The artwork becomes a bridge between the formless and the formed, between potential and actual.

This connects to what Socrates was doing when he sought universal objective moral truths. He wasn't just organizing existing knowledge; he was reaching toward something that transcended particular opinions and experiences. He interrogated people not to learn what they already knew but to help everyone discover what they didn't yet know they knew.

It also connects to Plato's concept of the Platonic realm—the realm of perfect forms. In this higher dimension of ideas, there exists an original, ideal version of everything. You see many chairs around you in the physical world, but somewhere there's an original, perfect "chair" from which all different chairs inherit their essential "chair-ness."

Every actual chair is an imperfect manifestation of the ideal Chair. The physical chairs we sit on are shadows of a more fundamental reality. When an artist creates something truly original, perhaps they're reaching into that Platonic realm and bringing back a piece of it—making visible something that was always true but never before seen.

Interestingly, this ancient concept shows up in modern programming. You can create classes and subclasses that inherit properties from parent classes. A general "chair" class might have subclasses with different properties: office chairs, rocking chairs, dining chairs, wheelchairs. All chairs have something to sit on, but the most abstract definition is simply "something you sit on."

Subclasses add specificity: feet, a back to lean on, armrests, four legs or three, wheels, a motor. Eventually a very complex "chair" becomes something closer to a vehicle. But they all inherit from that basic abstract concept of "something to sit on." The point is purpose and design, with subcategories branching out from a core idea.

This programming structure mirrors Plato's metaphysics remarkably well. There's a realm of perfect forms—the abstract classes—and variations trickle down from there into different specific types. The general precedes and defines the particular.

When artists create, they engage with this realm of forms. They don't just combine existing elements randomly; they're trying to manifest something that has a kind of prior existence in the space of possibilities. The artwork was always possible, but the artist makes it actual.

This is why truly original art feels like discovery rather than invention. The artist didn't create the form from nothing; they found it and brought it across. They were the first to see it, but in some sense it was already there, waiting to be seen.

This understanding of artistic creation as communication with the realm of forms elevates art beyond mere decoration or self-expression. The artist becomes a kind of mediator, making visible what was invisible, giving form to what was formless. They're not just showing us what is; they're showing us what could be, what wants to be, what's trying to emerge into being.

Not all art achieves this, of course. But the art that moves us most deeply, that feels genuinely revelatory, seems to be art that has successfully bridged the worlds—that has brought something through from the unknown and made it present.
