# The Fundamentals: Lines, Shapes, and Creative Freedom

All of this—the philosophy, the social function, the meditative practice—rests on a foundation of basic skills. Before you can communicate with the unknown or meditate through drawing, you must understand the fundamentals. And those fundamentals are simpler than you might think.

To draw something, you have to understand it first. To draw a house, you must understand that it's three-dimensional, with different faces interacting with the environment and light. There's a lot of 3D mapping happening in your mind before you even put pencil to paper. You're translating three-dimensional space into two-dimensional representation, which requires understanding how perspective works, how volumes occupy space, how surfaces relate to each other.

Understanding how lines work together, how they connect and create form—that's where it all begins.

Lines are the foundation of everything. In animation training, the first exercise is simple: draw circles, draw curves, draw whatever—just keep your hand moving. Find the right balance of pressure on the paper—not too hard, not too soft, but in the middle. Draw circles for at least five minutes. This develops control of lines, pressure, and shapes.

It sounds boring, but it's essential. Your hand needs to learn how to move smoothly, how to maintain consistent pressure, how to create the curves and angles you intend. This is muscle memory, built through repetition. Athletes practice basic movements thousands of times; artists do the same with lines.

Lines reveal more than you might think. You can tell how an artist thinks or processes information just by looking at their lines. You can read their experience, whether they're impulsive or controlled, how much pressure they naturally apply, whether they work quickly or slowly. There's even a practice of analyzing personalities through signatures and handwriting called graphology. Lines reveal a great deal about the person behind them.

After lines come shapes. Think in terms of 3D mapping using the most fundamental shapes: circles, triangles, rectangles. Everything is made of these fundamental shapes—you can find them in anything you look at. A human figure can be broken down into cylinders, spheres, and boxes. A face is a sphere with smaller shapes attached. Start with these basic forms and build upon them, like building blocks.

It's not a 2D process but 3D. Draw shapes multiple times from different perspectives—draw a cube from various angles until you can visualize it rotating in your mind. Once you master these fundamentals, color theory comes later. First you need to understand form, volume, and spatial relationships.

Once you understand lines, shapes, volume, and eventually colors, you start to understand the higher layers of art. You can abstract using the fundamental skills you've learned. The basics become tools for expressing more complex ideas.

Then you can start to play—to impute intention in your drawings, to shape perspective with purpose and design. Understanding the fundamentals of art—lines, volume, colors, and their relations to each other and the environment—unlocks the abstract layers of art.

This is counterintuitive for some people. They think fundamentals constrain creativity, that focusing on basics will make their work rigid or formulaic. The opposite is true. Fundamentals don't constrain creativity; they enable it.

Think of language. Grammar and vocabulary are fundamentals. Having more words and understanding how to structure sentences doesn't limit what you can say—it expands your possibilities. Someone with limited vocabulary and poor grammar is actually less free to express themselves, not more.

The same applies to art. An artist who can't control their lines is limited in what they can draw. An artist who doesn't understand perspective can't create convincing spaces. An artist who hasn't mastered basic shapes struggles to build complex forms. The fundamentals are liberating because they give you tools to manifest what you imagine.

Mastery of fundamentals is what allows spontaneity to work. Jazz musicians can improvise because they've practiced scales and chord progressions until they're automatic. Skilled calligraphers can produce flowing, spontaneous work because they've trained their hands through countless repetitions. The spontaneity is real, but it's built on a foundation of disciplined practice.

The path to creative freedom leads through the fundamentals, not around them. Learn to draw circles until you can do it without thinking. Master perspective until it becomes intuitive. Understand color relationships until you can mix exactly what you envision. Then, and only then, can you truly play—because you'll have the tools to manifest whatever you imagine.
