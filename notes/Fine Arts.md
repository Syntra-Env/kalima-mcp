Fine Arts: Philosophy, Purpose, and Practice

Plato, Socrates, and the Distortion of Reality

Plato was skeptical of artists because he saw art as mimesis—imitation. In The Republic, he argued that paintings and drawings are twice removed from truth: they imitate the physical world, which itself is merely a shadow of the ideal Forms. He believed artists influenced people's minds and perspectives in ways that might not be true.

Plato wasn't alone in worrying about how ideas could be twisted. His concern echoes a broader debate in ancient Greece involving the Sophists—paid teachers of philosophy and rhetoric who were associated with moral skepticism and specious reasoning. The Sophists were known for using clever but fallacious arguments, and their approach to truth put them at odds with Socrates.

The core of that conflict was this: the Sophists believed truth was relative and subjective, focusing on practical success—similar to pragmatic theory, where truth is simply "whatever works." Socrates disagreed. He sought universal, objective moral truths through relentless questioning. He didn't charge for teaching and emphasized ethical living and self-examination. Socrates also spoke frequently of "the divine" and his personal daimonion—an inner voice he believed guided him toward truth.

Language vs Visual Arts

But if Plato didn't like artists because they distorted reality, what about words? People also shape reality through language—their perception is filtered through the words they use. Socrates himself was a master of words, teaching through questioning. So why did he think language was better than visual arts when both are filtered through the perception of whoever uses them?

Perhaps the Sophists' pragmatic mindset explains this. Socrates may have been a counter-argument to them: "You use language this way; I disagree. Here's a different approach." He introduced his method through language because that's what they used—he was meeting them on their own ground.

The cultural context matters as well. The Greeks were in love with science and philosophy. The arts were more supplementary—the core of their zeitgeist was philosophy, language, sciences, and mathematics. Words were central to their intellectual life in a way that painting and sculpture were not.

Still, there's a question about what Plato meant by "arts." To be a sculptor, one needed mathematics—understanding perspective and geometrical projections. How much math was actually involved, and did that relate to his argument about artists distorting reality?

From an artist's perspective, sculpting is more intuitive than mathematical. Michelangelo said you visualize the finished piece in your mind, and all you do is carve it out. It's like archaeology—the sculpture is already there in the stone; you're just revealing it. If that's how one of the greatest sculptors saw his craft, there probably isn't as much formal mathematics involved as one might think.

The Renaissance and the Use of References

This intuitive approach to art—visualizing before creating—raises another question: where does the vision come from? In medieval times, before proper references existed, artists drew from their own minds or copied older Byzantine stylizations. This is why much medieval art looks flat and formulaic by later standards. Without observing reality directly, artists were limited to recycling inherited forms.

Giotto di Bondone changed that in the late 1200s. He surpassed all his teachers and peers because he started observing real people and nature instead of copying older drawings. This sparked a revolution in art. Others realized why his work felt so alive and began studying from life too. Later, Cennino Cennini wrote Il Libro dell'Arte, one of the first practical artist's handbooks, which emphasized drawing from nature. Because these references were based on real observation, they produced far better results than copying stylized predecessors.

The lesson here is simple: things work better when you interact with reality as it is. You could say Giotto used an "ontopoetic" approach—engaging the world directly, using his eye, treating the world as an interlocutor with whom he was communicating.

There's a distinction worth noting: someone can study something precisely without being ontopoetic. An ontopoetic worldview applies to everything you perceive, whereas some people only apply that precision to their specific field of expertise.

The Purpose of Art

So what is art actually good for? Many people see it as just an experience—you see it and leave. But throughout history, art has served much deeper functions.

Much older art was religious. Egyptian hieroglyphs blended writing with visual imagery, and beyond the script itself, temple walls included elaborate depictions of gods, pharaohs, and rituals. Different traditions used art as visual representation of abstract metaphors—primarily for pedagogical, or teaching, purposes.

This makes sense when you consider how abstraction works. Most abstract metaphors have a physical root—they begin by referring to a physical object or phenomenon, then abstract concepts are superimposed or built on top. Much of humanity's progress has come through higher and higher abstractions. Visual arts were probably one of the first technologies or systems of abstraction.

Abstraction and Simplification

Think of art as a simplified abstraction of concepts too vast to deal with directly—a way to visualize, stamp, or blueprint complex ideas.

Abstraction includes simplification because it removes details, making something generic enough to apply in multiple contexts. You remove specifics and keep an empty template that can hold anything. It's like creating a container that different things can fit into.

Consider the very first cave drawings. Perhaps that's where the concept of community and the concept of the individual first emerged visually. Drawings of hunting animals may represent community cohesion—the fact that people must work together and coordinate to achieve goals and control their world. These were essentially social systems being documented.

This is why art served pedagogical purposes and generational knowledge transfer. Some animals can use tools, but humans can transfer that knowledge to the next generation. We can forward our knowledge—and that ability is crucial to civilization.

Communicating with the Unknown

Beyond teaching what's already known, fine art is also a way of communicating with the unknown. It's like being in a dream, trying to catch something out there and bring it here. Artists are visionaries—they have to be if they want to succeed at capturing something that doesn't yet have form.

This connects to what Socrates was doing when he sought universal objective moral truths. It also connects to Plato's concept of the Platonic realm—the realm of perfect forms. In this higher dimension of ideas, you see many chairs around you in the physical world, but there's an original, ideal chair from which all different chairs inherit their essential "chair-ness."

Interestingly, this ancient concept shows up in modern programming. You can create classes and subclasses that inherit properties from parent classes. A general "chair" class might have subclasses with different properties. All chairs have something to sit on, but the most abstract definition is simply "something you sit on." Subclasses add feet, a back to lean on, armrests, four legs or three, wheels, a motor—eventually a very complex "chair" becomes something closer to a vehicle.

The point is purpose and design, with subcategories branching out from a core idea. There's a realm of perfect forms, and variations trickle down from there into different types.

Social Aspects of Art

While art has these lofty philosophical functions, it has also always been entangled with social class. Certain art styles were associated with higher social classes, while others belonged to common people.

The art that survives is typically from higher classes—it's usually the elite's art that gets preserved. We don't have much "peasant art." Yet ironically, it's the common people who transfer knowledge from generation to generation. The crowd, the populace, really keeps culture alive through oral traditions, crafts, and folk practices.

But formal art was different—it was for the elite. Parchment and vellum were expensive, and later paper remained costly. Where would common people draw? Materials were scarce, and you had to know how to draw first. There were many barriers to entry.

This parallels the history of writing. At first, writing was limited to elite books—scriptures, government documents. Eventually, people started writing novels, and the repository of written knowledge expanded to include more voices. The same happened later with art, though art was even more expensive than writing because it required large canvases, many tools, and substantial resources.

Comic books may mark when art began to be democratized and mainstreamed—when everyone could access and produce visual stories. Movies followed, though they're more recent than we think. Hollywood was dominant until recently, but we're seeing that shift now. Video creation became accessible with the internet—before that, even if you made a video, where would you put it?

Technology helped enormously: cheaper cameras, cheaper equipment. Computers that once cost a million dollars and filled entire rooms are now affordable and portable.

This reveals something important: technology and art go hand in hand. Someone once said that writing is a technology—which means art is also technology. When we say art and technology are connected, they may really be two sides of the same coin that cannot exist without each other. A pencil is technology, even if we don't see it that way anymore because it seems so natural.

Art as Individual Practice

Beyond its social and philosophical dimensions, there's another aspect to fine arts that's often overlooked: the individual meditative practice of creating art. The process itself has value.

In sacred geometry, many say the best way to benefit from its insights is to actually draw it yourself. The secrets are in the process by which patterns are constructed. The finished image matters less than the act of drawing it—the process is the key to insight.

Similarly, in magical and esoteric traditions, practitioners draw symbols, pentagrams, and diagrams. These are often ontological diagrams—models of the universe. For example, the Kabbalistic Tree of Life shows different dimensions of existence and how they relate to each other. You have to draw these to be initiated into the secrets they contain.

In a sense, this parallels what cave drawings did. Cave drawings transferred generational knowledge openly; esoteric drawing traditions do the same more secretively, guarding hidden knowledge through the discipline of practice.

Some contemporary artists describe their work in similar terms. They say it's not about what they're drawing—it's about controlling the lines and breathing. One extremely precise artist explained that his master taught him to breathe properly while drawing. He treats drawing as a breathing exercise, like meditation. The breathing helps him enter a flow state, which helps him process things psychologically.

For these practitioners, fine arts aren't about cultural status or social markers. They're individual practice for health, self-care, and self-exploration.

Carl Jung understood this dimension of art deeply. He drew extensively, most notably in his Red Book, also known as Liber Novus, which contains elaborate mandalas and symbolic imagery. Some of his drawings feature cross-like structures similar to cosmological diagrams from Mayan and Tibetan traditions. The Mayan World Tree, for instance, is often depicted connecting different realms of existence, with some representations emerging from or contained within a head—imagery that Jung would have recognized as archetypal.

Jung's work demonstrates how fine arts can serve as a vehicle and channel for abstract understanding. Whether for meditative purposes or psychological integration, the practice of drawing these images—not just looking at them—unlocks something.

Many esoteric traditions echo this: you have to draw these images to actually understand them. Especially with geometrical designs involving many lines, perfect curves, and circles, you must draw in very specific ways. You only figure that out when you try to draw them yourself.

Fundamentals of Art

All of this—the philosophy, the social function, the meditative practice—rests on a foundation of basic skills. To draw something, you have to understand it first. To draw a house, you must understand that it's three-dimensional, with different faces interacting with the environment. There's a lot of 3D mapping happening in your mind before you even put pencil to paper.

Understanding how lines work together, how they connect—that's where it all begins.

Lines are the foundation of everything. In animation training, the first exercise is simple: draw circles, draw curves, draw whatever—just keep your hand moving. Find the right balance of pressure on the paper—not too hard, not too soft, but in the middle. Draw circles for at least five minutes. This develops control of lines, pressure, and shapes.

Lines reveal more than you might think. You can tell how an artist thinks or processes information just by looking at their lines. You can read their experience, whether they're impulsive, how much pressure they apply. There's even a practice of analyzing personalities through signatures and handwriting called graphology. Lines reveal a great deal about the person behind them.

After lines come shapes. Think in terms of 3D mapping using the most fundamental shapes: circles, triangles, rectangles. Everything is made of these fundamental shapes—you can find them in anything you look at. Start with these and build upon them, like building blocks.

It's not a 2D process but 3D. Draw shapes multiple times from different perspectives—draw a cube from various angles. Once you master these fundamentals, color theory comes later.

Once you understand lines, shapes, volume, and colors, you start to understand the higher layers of art. You can abstract using the fundamental skills you've learned. The basics become tools for expressing more complex ideas.

Then you can start to play—to impute intention in your drawings, to shape perspective with purpose and design. Understanding the fundamentals of art—lines, volume, colors, and their relations to each other and the environment—unlocks the abstract layers of art. The fundamentals don't constrain creativity; they enable it.
