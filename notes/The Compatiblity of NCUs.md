# The Compatibility of NCUs

> **Prerequisites:** This note assumes familiarity with the [[NCU Framework and Interpretation]]. For context on the interpretive methodology, see [[Quran Self-Centric Interpretation]].
>
> **Related:** [[Angelic Possession]] | [[Discomfort and Spiritual Transformation]] | [[Channeling Session]]

The books of Tafseer create personalities that elevate characters like Ibrahim and Muhammad above the level of ordinary humans. Yet the Quran gives us descriptions and indications that show us how human they are. Interestingly, it is through this humanity that we are able to gain even more insights, or get insights in the first place, about their role beyond their life on this earth. Isn't that interesting? This observation becomes crucial when we consider the central tension I'm wrestling with here.

After the introduction to the NCU and the imperative to withhold any assumptions about how anything works outside of the NCU, I found myself in a difficult position when it came to understanding and thinking about afterlife characters. It may very well be that there is nothing similar between us and them, that the similarities are simply translations of an incomprehensible reality into terms that we could comprehend. But instead, I have come to submit to specifically the comprehensibility of afterlife characters. Any meaningful engagement with the Quranic narratives requires this submission.

The question then becomes: how could we ever apply any psychological traits to them if they are truly incomprehensible? You may say that they were once Earthlings, but to be an Earthling, they must have been in their own NCU. Those universes may have had nothing in common with mine other than the fact that they are created by Allah. This is where the tension sharpens, between the imperative not to assume and the practical necessity of understanding.

Yet that commonality, creation by Allah, is the starting point. I believe all universes created by Allah must have something in common. They must be coherent, just as the different parts of the Quran are coherent. But the other voice kept insisting that this coherence may not be based on the same principles that I'm familiar with. This is a valid objection, one I can't easily dismiss. Whatever it is they went through, however, I have come to understand that the journey to Allah follows a similar flavor. The concept of locution becomes especially attractive in this discussion, and perhaps offers a way forward.

Locution, as it is presented in the Quran, demonstrates a conceptual framework agnostic of representational system or language. It is, however, still rooted in the same or similar metaphors. This raises a deeper question: I wonder whether these metaphors could have been something different in the original language of their speakers, and what this would mean or what this should indicate to us when reading these passages. If the metaphors themselves are universal, this suggests a deeper compatibility between NCUs than the strict imperative would allow.

But here another paradox emerges. I had once asked whether my universe existed before I was born and will exist after I die. And I was answered: no. This was, of course, in the context of the NCU, but its implications reach far and wide. If this is the case, then why do we even speak about progeny? So often amongst us it is said that what we do is for future generations, to seek divine guidance. Yet there are no future generations to speak of if we speak of this earthling life. Then, of course, we must be of different NCUs—although we were instructed not to think or make any assumptions whatsoever on the nature of other universes. The contradiction seems unavoidable.

And yet, I must say that given the coherence of the stories in the Quran when traced through the steps of different characters—given that these characters are in their separate universes—this indicates to me that these different universes must be conceptually compatible. The narratives wouldn't hold together otherwise. The Quran seems to demand that we recognize this compatibility, even as the NCU framework warns against assuming it.

This is no final statement, however, as we are beginning to learn that the majority of the Quran refers primarily to the afterlife. If practically the entirety of the Quran is afterlife-focused, then inter-universe narratives may not be necessary at all, given that human-to-angel communication is quite more natural. Perhaps the resolution lies here, in recognizing that what appears to be inter-NCU communication is actually communication within a shared afterlife reality.

And yet these angels we speak of are assumed to have had an earthly existence, which brings us back to the original problem. I hear you saying that I am overcomplicating things, that I should look at these narratives less logically, that I should take advantage of the humanization of these narratives, for it is what makes them comprehensible. From this human perspective, insights are derivable. I hear you, and yet I still think that these questions are important—and not only important, but might definitively lead to a new, abstract understanding. The tension between logical rigor and practical comprehension may itself be instructive, pointing toward a mode of understanding that transcends both.
