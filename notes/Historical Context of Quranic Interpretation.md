# Historical Context of Quranic Interpretation

> **Prerequisites:** None (provides historical context)
>
> **Related:** [[Quranic Word Root Analysis]] | [[Story of Yusuf]] | [[NCU Framework and Interpretation]]

For the past 1400 years, the Islamic body of knowledge has been largely based on appeals to authority and tradition. Interpretations became established upon the transmitted reports of Muhammad, although more than 90% of the Quran has been left unexplained by him, and most if not all the clues he left us are cryptic.

Ali Ibn Ahmed Ibn Ibrahim Al-Maha'imi (d. 1432) was the first to take a systematic intra-Quranic approach to Quran interpretation. Before that, most Muslim scholars would interpolate the meaning of Quranic narratives based on the similarities that they held with ancient wisdom that they knew and loved, and less on making sure the their way of understanding the words was consistent for all instances.

A full concordance of the Quranic roots/word forms really only appears in the late 19th-early 20th century, with works like Flugel's Concordantiae Coranic Arabicae (1842) and Muhammad Fu'ad 'Abd al-Baqi's al-Mu'jam al-Mufahras li-Alfaz al-Quran, (1945). It was therefore difficult before that to systematically verify the validity of their perceived meaning of a word for all its instances. And consistency is necessary of a coherent text. 

In some cases, the context in which the word is found makes a chosen meaning awkward, so they looked for another meaning that would fit better in that instance. They referred to the life of the prophet and biblical stories as sources of understanding. When a story was "obviously" the same as its seemingly prophetic or biblical counterpart, then it was obvious to understand it in that context. This was the focus. So when they came upon the Chapter of Yusuf, this is what they saw:

a. [رَٰوَدَتْهُ] --> [seduce]
b. [غَلَّقَتِ ٱلۡأَبۡوَٰبَ] --> [she locked the doors]
c. [إِنْ هَٰذَآ إِلَّا مَلَكٞ كَرِيمٞ] --> [he could be but an angel] --> [handsome]
d. [ٱلسِّجۡن] --> [prison]

[a,b,c,d] --> [Joseph and Potiphar's wife]

If the same words are examined without assuming the biblical parallel, a different reading emerges:

a'. [رَٰوَدَتْهُ] --> [compel] — The same root appears in 12:61 where the brothers say "سَنُرَٰوِدُ عَنْهُ أَبَاهُ" (we will compel his father). They were not planning to seduce Yakub. The verb indicates pressure from leverage.

b'. [غَلَّقَتِ ٱلۡأَبۡوَٰبَ] --> [shed all pretexts] — The plural *al-abwāb* can mean pretexts. Later in 12:25, "وَٱسْتَبَقَا ٱلْبَابَ" they race toward *al-bāb* (singular)—one physical door. If she had locked multiple doors, why race toward one?

c'. [إِنْ هَٰذَآ إِلَّا مَلَكٞ كَرِيمٞ] --> meaning uncertain when examined across instances

d'. [ٱلسِّجۡن] --> [political consequence] — imprisonment for refusing to collaborate, not a lover's revenge

[a',b',c',d'] --> [spy recruited into conspiracy against Hyksos rulers, woman as conspirator compelling him to betray secrets]

Additional Quranic evidence supports this reading:

- 12:21 says "الَّذِي اشْتَرَاهُ مِن مِّصْرَ" (the one who bought him *from* Misr)—the preposition *min* rather than *fi* indicates the man was *from* Misr but conducted the purchase elsewhere, seeking Levantine slaves who spoke the Hyksos language.

- 12:21 "أَوْ نَتَّخِذَهُۥ وَلَدًا" — The expression *ittakhadha waladan* is defined in 39:3-4, where Allah explains that those who "ittakhadhu" intermediaries between themselves and Allah have designated agents, not adopted children. The man intended Yusuf as an intermediary agent in conspiracy.

- 12:21 describes her as "امْرَأَتُهُ" (*imra'atuhu*)—his subordinate woman, not his wife or partner. The term indicates hierarchy, not marriage.

- 12:25 "وَقَدَّتْ قَمِيصَهُۥ مِن دُبُرٍ" — His shirt was *slit* (قَدَّتْ) from behind, not torn. The verb *qadda* indicates a precise cut with a sharp object—she attacked him with a blade.

- 12:24 "لَوْلَآ أَن رَّءَا بُرْهَٰنَ رَبِّهِۦ" — The *burhan* (proof) he saw was physical evidence: the slit shirt that could testify on his behalf without him having to speak and expose his deception before the king.

- 12:21 "وَٱللَّهُ غَالِبٌ عَلَىٰٓ أَمْرِهِۦ" — Allah prevails over "his corrupt undertaking" (أَمْرِهِ). The same expression appears twice when Yakub describes corrupt decisions. This hardly describes a kind adoptive father.

This pattern—external assumptions overriding internal consistency—pervades traditional tafsir. Without concordances before 1842, scholars relied on hadith, Israeliyyat, pre-Islamic poetry, and consensus. Wherever a word resembled something familiar, that familiarity was assumed to be the meaning.

Other examples: systematic analysis shows every Quranic *dua* uses "Rabb," not "Allah"—the five instances of "Allah" in supplication are statements by rejectors. The word "jin" may refer to a type of hidden human, not spirits. "Azwāj" may indicate spiritual counterparts rather than literal spouses.

Dr. Hany Achan's Marvelous Quran channel presents the "organic Quranic methodology": words defined within the Quran, nested interpretation where terms illuminate each other, the Abrahamic locution with its specialized vocabulary, and systematic verification across all instances. The challenge is 1,400 years of inherited assumptions and the difficulty of admitting that established interpretations may be wrong.

Digital tools like the Kalima project could make concordance-level analysis accessible to all. Evidence-based understanding would replace appeal-to-authority. The Quran's internal coherence would become visible, and sectarian divisions rooted in differing external sources would diminish.
